English Grammar Guide: Present Perfect

1) Present Perfect

A) Usage: We use the present perfect for finished actions at an unspecific time in the past with consequences in the present.

Form:

Auxiliary verb have/has + Past Participle (verb +ED for regular verbs; irregular verbs).

üìù Examples:

  • I have eaten a lot. I think I need some tea.
  • Where's Emily? She has just gone out for lunch.
  • I've bought a new car. You must see it! It's incredible!
  • I've just arrived in Rio and I'm in love with this place.
  • She hasn't finished her homework yet, so she can't go out now.
  • Have you been to the new restaurant!? I've heard it's really good!
  • Has she drunk all the beer? Why?

2) Already / Yet / Just

A) Already & Yet: Used to give emphasis to actions before the moment of speaking. The action is finished before expected (already) or not finished at this moment (yet).

Word Sentence Type Meaning
Already Affirmative Finished before expected.
Yet Negative / Question Not finished / Ask if finished.
Just Affirmative Recently finished (short time ago).

üìù Examples:

  • Already: I've already finished my homework, so I have a lot of time to spare!
  • Already: She's only 16, but she has already entered university!
  • Yet (Neg): I haven't finished my homework yet, so I can't go out now.
  • Yet (Quest): Have you finished your homework yet? I'm waiting for you.

3) Simple Past vs. Present Perfect

üìÖ Simple Past

Focus: When it happened.

"I saw the pyramids when I went to Egypt."

Expressions to use:

  • Yesterday
  • Last week
  • That day
  • One day
  • When I was a child

Expressions to use:

  • Ever / Never
  • Once / Twice
  • A few times / Many times
  • So far / Yet
