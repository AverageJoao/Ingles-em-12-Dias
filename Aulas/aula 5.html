English Masterclass: Present Continuous & Possessives

1) Present Continuous

We use the present continuous for:

a) Actions at the moment of speaking:

  • I'm looking for an Archaeology book.
  • Be quiet! You're speaking too loudly!
  • The girl in red is reading the book now.
  • We are studying for a math test.

b) Temporary actions:

  • My teacher is organizing a seminar about Archaeology.
  • He's working at the library this month.

A) Affirmative

  • I'm studying English right now, Mom.
  • She's reading a fashion magazine.
  • They're going to class at this moment.

B) Negative

  • I'm not talking loudly. I am at the library.
  • She's not handing in her book. It's her boyfriend's.
  • We're not writing, we are typing.

Note: The contractions "isn't" and "aren't" are also possible, but are used less.

C) Interrogative

Q: What am I doing? Is she reading a book in the library?

A: Yes, she is / No, she's not

Q: Are they working on their school project together?

A: Yes, they are / No, they're not

2) My / Your / His / Her / Its / Our / Their

The possessive adjectives point to who possesses a certain object.

Subject Possessive Adjective
Imy
Youyour
Hehis
Sheher
Itits
Weour
Theytheir

Examples:

She: Rebecca is not reading her book at the library.

He: Richard doesn't know the title of his book.

They: Jane and Jack love their Archaeology teacher.

It: I have an ebook and its cover is pink.

3) 's possessive case

We use 's beside a name to indicate he/she is the owner of the object.

I like to drive David's car.

(David is the owner of the car, the person who possesses it.)

Hey, whose cell phone is this? → It's my father's phone.

("Whose" questions who the owner is. My father is the owner of the phone.)

Oh my God! I think Debbie's apartment is bigger than John's house! It's huge!

(Debbie has an apartment and John has a house.)

Attention!

The possessive is also used when talking about animals:

  • "Tommy? Is that your cat's name?" (The name of the cat is Tommy).
  • "He is painting his dog's house." (The dog is the owner of the house).

But it isn't used in cases between things/objects:
